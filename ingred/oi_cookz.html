Avoid for All Frying and Baking

The fast food industry found these totally unusable because they started to become rancid immediately upon heating and were completely unusable within a few hours (beef tallow was so stable it lasted a month). Products of rancidity are suspected carcinogens. If these oils aren't good enough for Burger King why would you use them?

  • Corn Oil   -   stability index 6.2
  • Safflower Oil   -   stability 7.6
  • Soybean Oil   -   stability 7.0
  • Sunflower Oil   -   stability 6.8
  • Canola Oil   - stability 5.5 - highly controversial.
  • "Vegetable Oil"   -   (whatever oil is cheapest at the time)
  • Other oils high in polyunsaturated fats.

High Temperature Frying Oils

All these oils will go to at least 450°F/235°C safely and are resistant to rancidity. All oils safe at these temperatures are refined oils except Avocado which may be virgin.

  • Olive Pomace Oil   -   460°F/240°C - affordable and excellent, with very low polyunsaturated content. stability 1.5.
  • Avocado Oil   -   520°F/270°C - excellent but less available, less economical and a little buttery in flavor.
  • Almond Oil   -   495°F/255°C - expensive.
Acceptable for shorter jobs, but more prone to oxidation (rancidity).
  • Peanut Oil   -   450°F/235°C - stability 3.7.
  • Rice Bran Oil   -   490°F/255°C - stability 4.2

Medium Temperature Frying Oils

These oils will go safely to at least 400°F/200°C and again are all refined oils.

  • Pure Olive Oil   -   (not virgin) - oxidation 1.5.
  • Coconut Oil   -   (refined) - oxidation 0.24
  • Palm Oil   -   refined.
  • Mustard Oil   -   labeled "for massage only" due lack of FDA certification, but used for cooking anyway.
  • Beef Tallow   -   oxidation 0.86.
  • Ghee   -   highly clarified butter.
  • Any of the higher temperature oils.

Moderate Temperature Frying Oils

All these oils will go safely to at least 350°F/175°C.

  • Pure Olive Oil   -   (not virgin) - oxidation 1.5.
  • Coconut Oil   -   (unrefined) - considered by some the most healthy oil - oxidation 0.24.
  • Lard   -   pig fat - oxidation 1.7.
  • Goose, Duck and Chicken fat.
  • Any of the higher temperature oils.

Low Temperature Frying Oils

These oils will all go safely to 300°F/150°C but may lose flavor before they reach that temperature, so 275°F/135°C is a good target maximum. Flavor is generally a major feature (and justification for higher price) with low temperature oils so you don't want to loose it.

  • Virgin Olive Oil.
  • Butter.
  • Sesame Oil   -   (unrefined).

Mayonnaise and Emulsified Sauces

  • Grapeseed Oil.
  • Pure Olive Oil   -   (virgin may be too strongly flavored).

Salad Oils

  • Virgin Olive Oil
  • Pure Olive Oil   -   not as strongly flavored as virgin.
  • Grapeseed Oil
  • Sesame Oil   -   (light and dark)
  • Various "Natural", "Cold Pressed" and "Virgin" oils sold for their unique flavors, including oils high in polyunsaturated fats (if properly stored).

Reusing Oil

Oils with a low oxidative stability index (1.5 and lower) can be reused several times provided they have not been overheated (to smoking or higher), they are filtered clean after use, and not too much time passes between uses. Oils with a higher oxidation index (polyunsaturated oils) should never be reused as they degrade rapidly.

After using a stable oil for deep frying I often reuse it a bit at a time for pan frying (if the flavor remains neutral). Oil used to fry fish should not be reused for any other purpose because it will become strongly flavored from dissolved fish oils, and these oils are not temperature stable.

To filter oil, line a wire strainer with a non-colored paper towel and pour the oil through it. Preferably it should still be fairly hot so it filters quickly. In some cases the filter will clog and have to be replaced before all the oil is filtered.

Sticking Problems

Often problem foods that stick to the pan with most oils will not stick if fried in Lard. Yes, the American Heart Association has vilified lard and made you fear it, but in the 19th century America practically lived on pig fat and congestive heart disease was almost unknown. Populations using a lot of lard and people on the Atkins diet have not experienced an elevated incidence of heart disease. There seems to be something amiss with AHA "science".

Cleanup Problems

It is best to grease baking pans and the like with lard or beef tallow. Vegetable oils dry quickly to varnish and become very difficult to clean off. If oil is not baked on too hard, paint thinner may be useful for removing it - but be very cautious of the fire hazard.
