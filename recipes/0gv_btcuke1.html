(Note-7) 		Make   -   (1+ hrs - 30 min work)
  1. Soak BEAN THREADS in hot water (a little too hot for you to hold a finger in). As always with food it's better to heat cold water than to use hot tap water. Let soak for 20 minutes or a bit more. Drain and chill in cold water, then drain again. Stretch out on the cutting board and cut into lengths of about 3 inches.
  2. Cut CUCUMBERS into strips about 1/8 inch square and 2 inches or so long. A julienning vegetable peeler is invaluable for this job. You could also use the coarse side of a box grater, but crosswise slices don't blend so well with the noodles (see Note-2).
  3. Chop CHILIS very fine and add to Cucumbers.
  4. Mix All Dressing Items, making sure the sugar is dissolved (I run it to powder in my spice grinder so it dissolves faster).
  5. Mix together ALL Items and allow to rest for as long as you can, turning now and then. If in a hurry, let sit at room temperature and then chill for 1/2 hour or more in the fridge. Overnight in the fridge is better. The longer it rests the better the flavors will blend and the chilis will be less sharp.
  6. Garnish as desired (Note-7) and serve.
NOTES:

  1. Bean Threads:

      Around here thin bean threads are most commonly in hanks of 1-3/4 t 2 ounces weight, but other forms are also seen, including bulk and 2 ounce hanks. DO NOT confuse these with similar looking Rice Sticks - totally different. For details see our Asian Noodles page.

  2. Cucumbers:

      Persian or similar cucumbers which need neither peeling nor seeding are preferable, but if all you have is waxed blimps peel them, split lengthwise and seed them. If you're using a julienning peeler you can just peel (with a regular peeler), then use the julienning peeler all around until you hit the seed mass.

  3. Green Chilis:

      Thai Chilis are a natural here. 3 will make this salad quite sharp, and 1 will not be quite mild, so use your own best judgement. Serranos can also be used, and are not as hot, but they've gotten rather large lately, and vary in hotness. I'd start with a half of one and see how it goes. You can always add more. For details see our Thai Chilis page.

  4. Chili Garlic Sauce:

      The almost universally available Huy Fong Chili Garlic Sauce will do fine, or anything like it.

  5. Black Vinegar:

      Chinese Chinkiang black vinegar is easily available these days. Pearl River Bridge is a good brand. If you don't have it, an "industrial" balsamic vinegar can be substituted, or you can use regular rice vinegar for a lighter color and flavor. For details see our Vinegars and Souring Agents page.

  6. Palm sugar:

      If you don't have this you can use Turbinado or some other lightly refined sugar.

  7. Garnish:

      Raw Peanuts, dry pan roasted and crushed, are a very popular garnish in Southeast Asia. For details see our recipe Dry Roasted Peanuts. I'm not that fond of peanut garnishes, so I usually garnish with cilantro leaves or threads of Fresno chili.
  8. U.S. measure: t=teaspoon, T=Tablespoon, c=cup, qt=quart, oz=ounce, #=pound, cl=clove in=inch, ar=as required tt=to taste
